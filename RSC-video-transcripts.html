Introduction to climate resilience

Speaker: Dr Lois Pennington

When we think about climate change, there is often a focus on mitigation, on cutting carbon emissions and net zero targets. But the impacts of climate change are already affecting communities around the world, and without interventions these impacts will worsen as we continue to emit greenhouse gases.

This challenge is all about climate resilience. That means our ability to cope with the changes that are already underway, and to prepare for what’s to come. We’ll explore how climate change is influencing extreme weather, who is most affected, and what it means to build resilience in a fair and inclusive way.

We’re going to focus on three key ideas. First, we’ll look at the link between climate change and extreme events, including how those events are changing over time and geography. Then we’ll explore the idea of adaptive capacity, in other words, who is able to respond and recover, and who isn’t. And finally, we’ll consider climate justice, the question of fairness in who is impacted, and how support is distributed.

Let’s start with the link between climate change and extreme events.

The world is getting warmer. Since pre-industrial times, global average temperatures have risen by around 1.2 degrees Celsius. But climate change doesn’t just mean things slowly heat up. It also means that weather is becoming more extreme, and more unpredictable.

Globally, the number of weather-related disasters has increased five-fold over the past 50 years, according to the World Meteorological Organisation. And those events are costing more, both in lives and in recovery efforts.

We’re seeing longer and more intense heatwaves, heavier rainfall leading to flash floods, and droughts that last for months or even years. Wildfires are becoming more frequent and more destructive. And storms are getting stronger, powered by warmer oceans.

Seasons are shifting. We’re seeing heatwaves in spring, floods in summer, and extreme weather patterns that don’t match historical expectations. And sometimes, multiple hazards happen close together, for example, a drought followed by a storm that causes landslides in dry, damaged soil. These overlapping events can be especially devastating.

This is part of what scientists call a shifting baseline. What was considered extreme 20 years ago may now be relatively normal. And that makes planning for infrastructure, for public health, for emergency services, much more complex.

While we can’t blame one specific storm or heatwave on climate change, we can say that climate change made that event more likely to happen, or more intense than it would have been otherwise.

So we know that extreme events are increasing, and that they’re changing. But their impact doesn’t just depend on the weather. It also depends on what’s already in place to respond to the event, and that brings us to our second key idea: adaptive capacity.

Adaptive capacity is the ability to prepare for, respond to, and recover from climate-related hazards. It depends on a range of factors including how strong our infrastructure is, how well our emergency services can respond, whether people have access to healthcare, housing, information, and support. It’s not just about buildings and defences, it’s about people, systems, and the resources available to them.

Take a heatwave as an example. In a city with good infrastructure, green space, and accessible healthcare, people may find it easier to manage the risks. But in another city, or even a different part of the same city, where housing is overcrowded, ventilation is poor, and services are limited, a heatwave can be deadly. In addition, vulnerable groups, such as elderly or disabled people, are often at greater risk than healthy individuals, simply because they may have fewer ways to stay cool, stay mobile, or access support.

So the same hazard can cause very different outcomes, depending on who’s affected. And those differences are not random. They reflect deeper inequalities, in income, in planning, and in access to support.

This leads us to our third and final key idea: climate justice.

Climate justice recognises that while climate change affects everyone, it doesn’t affect everyone equally, and that inequality is often the result of systemic issues. The people and places most at risk are often the ones who have the fewest resources to cope, and furthermore, who contributed least to the problem.

Globally, this includes low-income countries that are already facing the brunt of climate impacts, despite having tiny historical emissions. It includes Indigenous communities who are fighting to protect land and ecosystems, while being left out of decision-making. It includes cities and regions where past investment has been limited, and where people are already dealing with poverty, poor health, or insecure housing.

But climate justice also matters within wealthier countries. In the UK, for example, marginalised communities may live in areas more exposed to flooding or air pollution. They may have fewer options for relocation, less access to support, and less influence over local planning decisions. So when we talk about building resilience, we can’t just talk about defences, walls, and warning systems. We have to talk about fairness, about who is protected, and who is left behind.

Let’s look at how these ideas play out in the real world.

In early 2025, wildfires in Los Angeles displaced thousands of people. The flames didn’t distinguish between rich and poor neighbourhoods, but the aftermath did. Wealthier residents were more likely to have insurance, access to temporary housing, and the means to rebuild. Others were left without support or permanent shelter.

In the Marshall Islands in the Pacific Ocean, rising sea levels are already threatening entire communities. Some residents are making plans to relocate. These communities have contributed almost nothing to global greenhouse gas emissions, yet they are on the frontlines of the climate crisis, with limited capacity to limit impacts.

And in the UK, we’ve seen repeated flooding in areas around the country. Each year brings new images of submerged homes and emergency rescues. These events have highlighted how important local planning, infrastructure, and community support are in responding to climate impacts.

These stories are no longer unusual. They’re becoming part of everyday life, reminding us that resilience is not just about weather, but about systems, services, and fairness.

So, to summarise: climate change is already making extreme weather more common and more unpredictable. These events are changing in where they happen, when they happen, and how severe they are. The ability to cope with these events - adaptive capacity - varies hugely, depending on resources, services, and systems. And climate justice reminds us that how we implement resilience must be fair, not just effective.

As we plan for a warming world, we need to ask not only how we can reduce emissions, but also how we can protect people, and ensure that no one is left behind.

Resilience over time

Dr Chris Bowden

Climate resilience is about our ability to anticipate, prepare for, and respond to climate-related disruptions – from heatwaves to food shortages to extreme storms. But resilience doesn’t happen all at once or in the same way everywhere. It unfolds across different timescales – and these often reflect the spatial scale of action, from individual households to entire international communities.

In this video, we’ll explore how we build resilience across three key levels:

  • The short-term, local scale – where individual choices matter.
  • The short- to medium-term, regional scale – where collective action can reshape systems.
  • The long-term, international scale – where cooperation and justice define our future.

Let’s start by looking at the short-term resilience at the individual and local scale. Local actions are the front line of climate adaptation. The choices we make in our homes, neighbourhoods, and communities can have immediate effects – from reducing our exposure to climate risks, to strengthening local supply chains and social ties.

Examples include:

  • Choosing transport options that are less vulnerable to fuel shortages or flooding
  • Adopting seasonal, local diets to reduce reliance on global supply chains
  • Retrofitting homes to stay cool in heatwaves or retain heat in winter
  • Supporting local environmental initiatives like urban greening

These behavioural shifts may seem small, but they build momentum. Think of the UK's sharp rise in cycling infrastructure post-COVID – a response to public demand that’s now shaping urban policy and resilience planning.

For example, in Todmorden, West Yorkshire, the community-led initiative ‘Incredible Edible’ turned unused land into local food gardens. It increased food security, reduced reliance on imports, and fostered local engagement – a clear example of short-term, local resilience in action.

But while local action is essential, it’s only the first step. Without broader systems enabling and supporting these behaviours, progress can stall.

Next, let’s look at the short- to medium-term resilience at the regional and national level. When individuals act collectively – through councils, regional governments, or small-to-medium businesses – their ability to drive structural change increases substantially.

At this level, climate resilience is about adapting infrastructure and systems to withstand future conditions. Planning decisions about:

  • Green spaces for urban cooling
  • Flood-resilient infrastructure
  • Accessible, climate-safe transport networks
  • Building codes that consider future climate extremes

…can either empower or constrain local action.

For example, in Greater Manchester, the ‘In Our Nature’ initiative uses urban green infrastructure to track and reduce carbon emissions, improve access to nature, and strengthen social cohesion. It’s a regional example of scaling up resilience through coordinated action.

Councils and businesses also shape the cultural narrative – what we normalise, invest in, and expect from daily life. Their decisions ripple across decades, especially when it comes to urban planning, land use, and community resilience strategies.

And finally, some aspects of climate resilience require global collaboration over the long term. We’ve already seen that coordinated international agreements can drive real and rapid change – as with the global phase-out of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) under the Montreal Protocol. This treaty helped reverse damage to the ozone layer – a rare success story in global environmental governance.

But it also exposed deep climate justice issues. While all countries complied, the cost of change was unevenly distributed. Wealthier nations had the means to transition quickly, while poorer nations faced steeper relative costs.

We face similar challenges today with climate adaptation. The nations that contribute least to climate change often face the most severe impacts. For instance:

  • Rising cooling demands in tropical and subtropical regions
  • Higher vulnerability to floods, droughts, and food insecurity
  • Displacement due to sea level rise and extreme weather

These realities require sustained funding, technology transfer, and international cooperation to support climate-resilient development around the world.

For example, the ‘Green Climate Fund’, established under the UNFCCC, is designed to help developing countries respond to climate change. It’s an evolving mechanism to address both long-term resilience and global climate equity – though challenges around financing and accountability remain.

Resilience is not a single decision, but a journey across time and scale.

  • In the short term, individuals and communities are already taking meaningful action to adapt.
  • In the medium term, councils and businesses can reshape systems to support that adaptation.
  • In the long term, international cooperation must ensure both resources and justice are in place to protect the most vulnerable.

To build a truly resilient future, we need all levels of society working together – each reinforcing the other. Only then can we ensure that climate resilience is not just possible, but fair and enduring.

Adaptation and maladaptation

Speaker: Dr Ben Parkes

In the context of sustainability, adaptation means taking steps to reduce climate risks and vulnerability by adjusting existing systems. There are multiple types of adaptation and adaptations to each type of weather shock. In this video, we will focus on heatwaves as our weather shock, but the thought processes are equally valid for cold waves, extreme storms, or floods.

I don’t wish to get bogged down in formal definitions of groups, but instead provide some general types of adaptation to heat:

  • Infrastructural changes can include – air conditioning a building, building underground, adding shade to railway platforms.
  • Institutional changes can include – changing working hours, providing water to staff/customers.
  • Behavioural changes can include – turning on fans at home, keeping curtains closed in the hot part of the day, taking an extra shower, not doing high exertion exercise.
  • Nature-based adaptations can include – planting trees for shading, providing green space.

In previous videos we’ve looked at the timescale of adaptation methods; now we’re going to spend some time on the physical scale too.

So, going back to my initial example of a heatwave, what sort of things can individuals do during the heatwave?

They can use a fan, or perhaps if they’re well off, turn on the air conditioning. They could change behaviour and take extra showers or head to a shopping mall and enjoy the air conditioning there.

At the local or regional scale during the heatwave, decision makers can:

  • Make public transport free to reduce the number of cars in cities (one engine instead of many).
  • Distribute water to people and turn on municipal fountains.
  • Increase provision of emergency services to account for the uptake in illnesses during heatwaves.
  • Open up municipal buildings to allow people space to cool down.

At national or international scales, the reactive decisions are similar to the regional ones:

  • Bringing in water and emergency services.
  • Ensuring local power stations have adequate fuel to power the increased energy demand for space cooling.

As we covered earlier, as scale increases, so does the timespan ahead that you can plan. Individuals can only affect their own area; local councils can affect the region, and national governments interact with their international counterparts to form long-term plans.

With heatwaves in the future:

  • Individuals could invest in air conditioning or shades for their windows.
  • At regional level, a council can plant trees to increase shading or change the quality of steel in rails to prevent buckling for the local light rail system.
  • Nationally, a government can mandate maximum working temperatures or make agreements to purchase energy in the case of a future heatwave.

However, adaptation is not always of benefit to people. This is known as maladaptation and occurs when the adaptive method is no longer beneficial to the user.

In the case of a heatwave, a fan is an adaptive measure when the air temperature is lower than the skin temperature of the individual – typically around 36°C. Once air temperatures exceed this, a fan will only blow warmer air onto the person and increase their heat loading.

Maladaptation can happen at wider scales too. Using schools or other publicly owned buildings as a refuge from the heat only works if the buildings stay cool. Should the power supply fail, then a gym or shopping mall turns from a cool refuge into a furnace of stressed people.
